Spatial and temporal change of magma generating condition of High-Mg andesite in the SW Japan

Introduction

The Setouchi Volcanic Belt in the SW Japan arc has been studied by many reserchers because of its possibility to constrain the thermal state during subduction initiation of the Shikoku Basin. (Geological records such as exposure of igneous rocks and crustal structure are on land and they are spatially and temporary continuous.)

There are various types of magmatism including high-Mg andesite (HMA) and felsic to mafic volcanism. Although The magma generating condition of primary melt of HMA and mafic rock has been apploached from experimental (Tatsumi, 1982; Kawasaki et al., 1993) and geochemical (Shimoda et al., 1998; Shimoda & Tatsumi, 1999; Tatsumi & Hanyu, 2003) data, the exact chemical composition of the primary magma has not been 明らかになる. The possibility of existence of more primitive melt than erupted magma is indicated from orthopyroxenes which show oscillatory zoning and have primitive zone.

Discussion

Erupted magma is generally modified its primary composition by crustal processes such as fractional crystallization, magma mixing, crustal assimilation, and degassing. To estimate the chemical composition of the primary melt, it is required to remove these effects. Because lavas in studied area tend to show mingling structure and to include granitic xenoliths, the effect of magma mixing and crustal assimilation are need to be considered. In order to minimize the effects of magma mixing and crustal assimilation, we focused on the samples which have the highest concentration of MgO and lowest SiO2 (sample name: 1002-9-1 in the Central area; 1029-4 in the West margin). Although evidence of mixing or assimilation could be extincted by complete mixing, compositional zoning observed in phenocrysts are limited in primitive range.

If the effect of magma mixing and crustal assimilation is negligible, we can estimate the chemical composition, except for volatile component, of the primary melt by removing the effect of fractional crystallization. To do so, it is generally accepted that: (1) assuming Rayleigh fractionation; (2) assuming fractionated solid phases; (3) repeatedly adding small fraction of solid component being equilibrium with the melt and recalculate the melt composition; (4) terminating when melt composition reaches to adequately primitive one.

In this method, it is important to evaluate validation of each assumption. The most critical issue is the 4th assumption. In the previous studies, HMA lavas are considered to preserve primary composition because of their olivine phenocrysts show nomal zoning whose profile is equilibrium with whole rock composition in the core. However the possibility of existence of more primitive melt can not be eliminated because: (1) it is possible that more primitive crystals are completely removed from the melt; (2) more primitive zoning in olivine crystal is easily flattened by diffusion.

Assuming mantle olivine composition is not applicable because its composition can be modified in various degree through events related to plate subduction (ex. metasomatism and, or partial melting).

To overcome these difficulty we put light on the compositional zoning in orthopyroxene phenocrysts, which are commonly included in HMA lavas. Orthopyroxene tend to be composed of Cr2O3 and Al2O3 component and their diffusion late are slow. Because abundance of these component correlate with degree of differentiation and crystallization pressure, we can estimate the temporal change of melt composition and crystallization condition by analyzing chemical profile of orthopyroxene phenocrysts.
